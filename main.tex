\section{Introduction}

\hspace{1em}The main goal of this assignment is to study the damage mechanism implemented using the ductile damage model with
pre-determined displacement failure from the defined mesh size. The damage model is introduced by creating a criterion such that the
element deletion is achieved as the damage parameter reach D = 1. This measurement ensured the damage mesh element that underwent the damage is no
longer have the load bearing capability, which also influence the overall specimen as well. 

\hspace{1em}The task is divided into main three parts. The first one focuses on the material damage behavior and the comparison head to head between the
extracted stress-strain curve and the damage parameter. Hence, the origin of the stress triaxiality coefficient is traced using the equation
derivation from the hydrostatic and the equivalent stress depending on the stress state or the applied boundary condition in the specimen. 
The discussion continued with the determination of the key deformation stages from the curve, highlighting the main critical
event. A comparison of damage evolution and the stress-strain curve is also overviewed to see the mechanism of the damage development that accumulates
throughout the damage state.

\hspace{1em}Next task discusses the damage evolution and failure pattern in stress distribution manner. Hence, the damage propagation mechanism
is the main discussion, comparing as well on how does the stress distribution grow by observing stress-strain evolution side-by-side. This give understanding
on the damage mechanism itself in the geometry visual. The last task is mainly focuses on the influence of the shape and size of the hole to the
stress distribution and the flow curve performance for each variation.

\subsection*{Q1: Materal Damage Behavior}

\vspace{1em}
\begin{tcolorbox}[title=\textbf{Stress Triaxiality}]
\textit{$\dots$ Ductile damage in Abaqus requires an additional parameter that is not directly provided in the material data: stress triaxiality. Define this parameter, including its mathematical expression, and explain its physical meaning. Then, calculate the appropriate value of stress triaxiality for your simulation based on the given loading condition.}
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{1em}
To calculate the stress triaxiality, the basic formulation of such parameter is defined as following:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:StressTriaxiality}
  \eta = -\frac{p}{q} = \frac{\sigma_m}{\sigma_{eq}} = \frac{\frac{1}{3}(\sigma_1 + \sigma_2 + \sigma_3)}{\sigma_{eq}}
\end{equation}

\vspace{1em}
Where the numerator is derived from the hydrostatic pressure, which denote as $\sigma_m$.
Hence, the value of $p = -\sigma_m$ showing the hydrostatic pressure is the result of the mean
stress of the 3 components. The denominator $\sigma_{eq}$ is defined as the Von Mises stress, which
can be elaborated as follows:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:VonMises}
  \sigma_{eq} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2} \left[ (\sigma_{11} - \sigma_{22})^2 + (\sigma_{22} - \sigma_{33})^2 + (\sigma_{33} - \sigma_{11})^2 + 6(\tau_{12}^2 + \tau_{23}^2 + \tau_{31}^2) \right]}
\end{equation}

\vspace{1em}
Since the numerical model does not possessed shear stress components, the $\sigma_{ii}$ can be
simplified into $\sigma_{ii}$, and the only stress subjected to the geometry is $\sigma_{2}$, 
the derivation of the stress triaxiality can be done as proceeding:

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:simplified}
  \eta = \frac{\frac{1}{3}(0 + \sigma_2) + 0}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{2} \left[ (0 - \sigma_{2})^2 + (\sigma_{2} - 0)^2 + (0)^2 \right]}} = \frac{\frac{1}{3}(0 + \sigma_2) + 0}{\sigma_2} = \frac{1}{3}
\end{equation}

\vspace{1em}
From this derivation, the obtained stress triaxiality is \textbf{0.33}. Hence, this value will be used to further calculation
for all of the numerical models.

\subsection*{Q2: Damage Evolution and Failure Pattern}

\vspace{1em}
\begin{tcolorbox}[title=\textbf{Visualization}]
  \textit{...\begin{itemize}
    \item Visualize damage evolution at several time increments, by providing the contour plots.
    \item Describe the damage path and explain:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Why do certain elements fail earlier than others,
      \item How stress concentration influences the observed damage path
    \end{itemize} 
  \end{itemize}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{1em}

\hspace{1em} Figure~\ref{fig:Case1} shows the contour stress plot in S22 direction for case 1 with the marked time frame in
stress-strain curve visualized in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}. In the elastic region, denote in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}a,
S22 distribution shows a small dominance stress concentration near the circular hole tip both in left and right side. As the stress increase, the 
stress concentration begins to spread into larger area, which is shown in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}b. Shrinkage of the stress concentration
area afterward observed in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}c and~\ref{fig:Case1}d, where the largest stress area begins to shift from
the hole both corner. The first element deletion, indicating the initiation of damage stage, is observed in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}e. This element deletion
is considered as the damage path, as this feature triggered by the fulfillment of the damage criterion that already defined in the material properties. The damage 
continues to propagate shown in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}f -~\ref{fig:Case1}h, marking the final separation of
the upper and lower plate in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}i.

\hspace{1em} The early failure or sequential deterioration can be explained by the damage mechanism
in the plate with a hole. As it observed throughout the process, the highest stress concentration started from the tip of the hole. Hence, the stress increased firstly
for the element near the hole, where this area is considered as the area without a load bearing capability. This consequently accelerate the increase
of damage parameter to 1, which follows by the element deletion afterward. Since the element deletion exist, thus increase the area without load bearing capability. This shifted 
the stress concentration as the element deletion propagated subsequently throughout the plate as the element deletion
continues to propagate. Therefore, a step-wise failure is observed since the element needs some time to reach the maximum damage value.

\hspace{1em} The stress concentration plays a role in controlling the damage path direction. In this case, the stress concentration is built up in horizontal direction
with respect to the hole. The propagation of damage then follows the highest stress concentration factor, since higher stress will eventually initiate damage
faster, thus will trigger the element deletion criterion. This is proved in figure~\ref{fig:Case1}, as the damage
propagation/element deletion follows the highest stress concentration position. Additional figure~\ref{fig:Case1_Plot} shows the plotted point of the contour plot in~\ref{fig:Case1} for each case in the flow curve.

\hspace{1em}As shown in the flow curve figure~\ref{fig:ComparisonPlot}, three of the distinguished holes create different curve behavior. Where for case 2, it has
the largest fracture toughness, measured by the area below the curve and case 3 (horizontally-stretched hole) has the lowest fracture toughness. The fracture toughness correlate with the
damage initiation, as it is proved by case 2 that has the most delayed damage initiation time compare to the other 2 cases. Comparing the damage degradation evolution mechanism, 
case 1 have the flattest damage degradation, by examining the slope of the damage evolution from 0 to 1 compare to case 2 and case 3. 

\hspace{1em}If it is to be compared the damage path between all cases, case 2 have the unique damage behavior compare to the others. As seen in figure~\ref{fig:Case2}, the element separation initiates inside the plate instead of in the surface plate hole.
While case 1 and case 3 have the same damage initiation starting from the surface hole, putting a difference in the case 3 as it propagates earlier compare to case 1 according to figure~\ref{fig:Case1}. It is concluded that the hole with larger horizontal-stretched shape inside have the larger
tendency to have a faster crack propagation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/Q3_StressFlow.pdf}
    \caption{Stress flow for different hole variation (horizontally-stretched)}
    \label{fig:Stressflow}
\end{figure}

\hspace{1em}To explain the reason of such faster crack propagation in the larger horizontally-stretched hole, figure~\ref{fig:Stressflow} demonstrate the stress flow with the same applied stress 
for different hole variation and no-hole specimen. As shown at most left side of the plate, the stress flow follows straight line, spanning across the plate. The stress flow only interested in non-free surface. This will change the line as a hole is introduced, for example in the middle picture figure~\ref{fig:Stressflow}. The stress flow line is deviate, avoiding the free surface. Increasing sizes of the hole perpendicular to the stress line will deviate eventually the stress flow line, as the line is grouped tighter to the non-free surface (shown in figure~\ref{fig:Stressflow} right side). 
This leads a higher stress generation, which leads to higher tendency to generate crack faster and decisively.
